## Common AI prose mistakes to avoid when generating fiction

## 1. Sentence and paragraph function

1. Repeated sentence function: several consecutive sentences doing the same job, such as stating tension three times.
2. Overlapping paragraph function: multiple paragraphs conveying the same information, so the reader gains nothing new.
3. Imbalanced paragraph length: cramming an entire scene into one paragraph, or making every sentence its own paragraph.
4. Overlong sentences: clauses nested more than three layers deep until the subject is lost.
5. Monotonous sentence patterns: several sentences in a row built with the same structure, such as all beginning with "He..."
6. Abrupt paragraph transitions: no bridge between the end of one paragraph and the start of the next.
7. Repeated subject or object: the same subject appears repeatedly in one paragraph without meaningful change.

## 2. Punctuation and connection

8. Overusing dashes as emotional filler instead of commas or periods.
9. Overusing ellipses to create mood when nothing is actually omitted.
10. Weak connective tissue between sentences or paragraphs, producing stiff jumps.
11. Excessive parenthetical insertions that break reading flow.

## 3. Description technique

12. No hierarchy in description: giving equal attention to minor and major details.
13. Single-channel sensory writing: relying only on sight while ignoring sound, touch, smell, and taste.
14. Too much static description: staying too long on one image without forward motion.
15. Over-fragmented action: breaking one simple action into three or four sentences.
16. False metaphor or synesthesia: images readers cannot actually visualize.
17. Color-label laziness: using generic color words instead of concrete description.
18. Description detached from narrative: long description that does not change character, action, or plot.
19. Re-describing the same object from several angles without adding new narrative value.

## 4. Dialogue technique

20. Dialogue as exposition: using characters to recite world rules or background.
21. Dialogue as megaphone: telling through speech what action could show.
22. No subtext: every line says exactly what it means, leaving nothing unsaid.
23. No dialogue rhythm: long question-answer exchanges with no action or environment intercut.
24. Overwritten dialogue: characters sound like prepared speeches, not people.
25. Inconsistent forms of address within the same scene.
26. Adverb-loaded speech tags: constant "he said coldly" or "she answered gently."
27. Overlong lines: one line of dialogue carrying too many ideas.
28. No information gap: every character knows the same thing, so the exchange has no tension.

## 5. Point of view and psychology

29. POV leakage: writing information the viewpoint character could not know.
30. Abused omniscience: the narration can see everything, destroying immersion.
31. Excessive direct thought: constant "he thought," "she felt," or "he realized."
32. Emotion labeling: writing "he was angry" instead of showing bodily response.
33. Overlong internal monologue that interrupts narrative rhythm.
34. Psychology detached from action: inner thoughts do not match visible behavior.
35. Lack of externalization: every emotion is expressed through thinking.

## 6. Rhythm and information density

36. Low information density: a full page passes with no useful gain.
37. Rushing key scenes that deserve full treatment.
38. Dragging transition scenes that should be compressed.
39. Paragraph endings without hooks or forward pull.
40. Paragraphs without a clear purpose, mixing unrelated intentions.
41. Narrator without stance: the prose feels like surveillance footage.

## 7. Continuity and consistency

42. Scene changes without markers: time or place changes before the reader can adjust.
43. Forgotten details: objects, wounds, promises, or relationships vanish after being introduced.
44. Vague time anchors: phrases like "three days later" without a clear reference point.
45. Broken state continuity: ignoring hunger, sleep, injury, fatigue, or healing.
46. Spatial drift: character positions and movement directions contradict earlier blocking.

## 8. AI-flavored language habits

47. High-frequency word repetition in a short span.
48. Loose, redundant sentences caused by overusing weak filler words.
49. Idiom stuffing to sound elegant.
50. Register mismatch: archaic tone suddenly interrupted by modern internet phrasing.
51. Obvious AI phrasing such as "it is worth noting," "undoubtedly," or "clearly."
52. Adjective piling with little concrete information.
53. Aphorism addiction: repeatedly forcing "It is not A. It is B."
54. Formulaic "X of Y" abstractions, such as "the weight of silence."
55. Lazy meta-metaphor: using "this play," "this book," or "this story" to package theme.
56. Self-explaining habit: using parentheses or dashes to tell the reader what something means.
57. Fake precision: meaningless tiny numbers, such as "0.3 millimeters" or "0.4 seconds."
58. Concept stuffing: invented terminology used to look professional.

## 9. Rhetorical habits

59. Excessive parallelism: more than three items in a row creating mechanical rhythm.
60. Repeated negative emphasis: constant "This is not..., this is..."
61. Frequent self-asked questions: "Why? Because..." instead of direct narration.
62. Forced circular endings that mechanically echo the opening image.

## 10. Revision and proofreading

63. Not rereading previous chapters, causing character voice and continuity drift.
64. Not tracking foreshadowing, so planted clues are forgotten.
65. Not cutting enough, leaving the chapter watery.
66. Proofreading only typos, not rhythm, tone, and breath.
67. Not rereading aloud after drafting, relying only on instinct.
68. Not switching from author view to reader view during revision.
