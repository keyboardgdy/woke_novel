# 12 Core Features of Human Writing

Writing that reads like AI usually fails not in grammar or logic, but in the texture that human writing carries naturally. This document is both a diagnostic checklist and a repair guide.

---

## Quick-Reference Table

| Feature | Human Expression | Typical AI Weakness |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Personal experience** | Specific private memories and scenes | Generalized "imagine a scene" material |
| **Emotional granularity** | Contradictory, complex, uncertain feelings | Clear but shallow emotion labels |
| **Sensory detail** | "Burnt coffee mixed with the rubber smell of a subway" | Abstract sensory adjective piles |
| **Cognitive imperfection** | Digressions, self-correction, associative leaps | Seamless logical chains |
| **Point-of-view bias** | Strong personal stance, even partial or extreme | Balanced "on one hand, on the other hand" phrasing |
| **Cultural code** | In-group references, era memory, subculture markers | Generic popular-culture references |
| **Language quirks** | Catchphrases, odd metaphors, sentence preferences | Textbook-standard expression |
| **Time-space anchors** | "Last winter in a Beijing hutong" | Vague "somewhere" settings |
| **Irregular rhythm** | Extreme alternation of long and short sentences, breaks, fragments | Even sentence patterns and steady breath |
| **Real vulnerability** | "I am not sure, but I think..." | Confident declarative statements |
| **Humor and irony** | Context-dependent wordplay and cultural nuance | Surface-level cleverness |
| **Process trace** | Marks of hesitation, reversal, and drafting | Too polished, with no sense of process |

## Feature-by-Feature Techniques and Examples

### 1. Personal Experience — Replace Public Imagination with Private Memory

**Core problem**: AI tends to write "scenes anyone could imagine," lacking the texture of memories that belong to one specific person.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- Assign each character 2–3 intensely private sensory memories (a childhood afternoon, the exact feel of a specific failure); let them surface naturally at emotional peaks
- Memories should contain "errors" — misremembered timing, conflated events, mixed-up faces. That's the signature of real recall
- Recollection is fragmentary: a single image, one sentence someone said, a smell

**❌ Typical AI**:
> She thought back to her childhood. It had been a happy time, with warm sunlight and the scent of flowers in the air.

**✅ Human texture**:
> She kept coming back to that afternoon — fourth grade, maybe fifth — Gran standing at the stove, flour on her apron, and the oil just exploded for no reason. Gran swore in dialect, something she never caught the meaning of, but the melody of that curse? She could still hum it thirty years later.

### 2. Emotional Granularity — Feelings Are Never Just One Thing

**Core problem**: AI labels emotions — "she was sad," "he felt angry." Real human feelings are several emotions tangled together, often impossible to name cleanly.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- Any important emotional scene must layer at least two contradictory feelings (relief laced with self-disgust; happiness for a friend with acid in the stomach)
- Let characters be confused by their own reactions — "I couldn't tell if it was grief or relief"
- Replace emotion names with body responses: don't say "afraid," write "knees going soft, fingernails digging into palms"

**❌ Typical AI**:
> Hearing the news, an indescribable wave of sadness washed over her. Tears blurred her vision.

**✅ Human texture**:
> She found herself laughing. No — her mouth was twitching. She read the text three times and it meant something different each time. She put the phone face-down, went to the kitchen, poured a glass of water, drank, realized it was cold, dumped it. Not sad. Really not sad. Just — knees a little soft.

### 3. Sensory Detail — Specific, Cross-Wired, Memory-Loaded

**Core problem**: AI piles on adjectives ("warm sunlight," "fresh air"). Human senses are specific, anchored to memory, and sometimes cross-wired between modalities.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- Sensory descriptions must be specific enough to identify place and time: "iodine smell" beats "pungent odor" by a hundred miles
- Use synesthesia and cross-modal bleed: sounds that have color, smells that have shape, textures that feel like a scene
- Commit to 1–2 senses per moment and go deep; don't run through all five like a spec sheet

**❌ Typical AI**:
> The air was filled with the scent of flowers. Warm sunlight bathed everything. Distant music drifted through the gentle breeze.

**✅ Human texture**:
> The clinic always smelled the same — iodine and leather seats baking in the sun. As a kid she'd assumed every hospital in the world used the same floor wax.

### 4. Cognitive Imperfection — Let the Narration "Err"

**Core problem**: AI narration is logically airtight and smoothly transitioned, like an essay through its eighth revision. Real thought is full of detours, backtracking, and self-contradiction.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- In first-person or deep POV, allow the narrator to self-correct: "No, that's not right. What I mean is…"
- Characters think tangentially — worrying about dinner while planning a heist, thinking about a mortgage while missing someone
- Show characters hesitating, reversing, rationalizing, talking themselves into decisions

**❌ Typical AI**:
> He carefully analyzed the situation, weighed the pros and cons, and decided to act.

**✅ Human texture**:
> He should call the police. Right, call them. But his phone stayed in his pocket — not because he couldn't find it, his fingers just wouldn't work. Hold on. What if he was wrong? The sound seemed to have stopped. He pressed his ear to the wall. No. Still there. Like fingernails on a chalkboard but duller. Call or don't?

### 5. Point-of-View Bias — Every Narrative Voice Has an Attitude

**Core problem**: AI defaults to neutral, objective, multi-perspective fairness. But fiction is not journalism — every character sees the world through tinted glass.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- The narrating POV must carry bias: the same event told by a different character would filter different details and pass different judgments
- A character's description of someone else reveals the describer's values, not objective truth
- Let characters (especially first-person narrators) make cognitive errors, overgeneralize, refuse to admit fault

**❌ Typical AI**:
> Both sides had valid points. On one hand, his concerns were reasonable; on the other, her persistence was understandable.

**✅ Human texture**:
> Here she goes again. Every time this topic comes up, out comes the victim logic — like the whole world owes her something, like he's sitting here not to solve the problem but to perform an apology.

### 6. Cultural Code — Build Authenticity with In-Group Shorthand

**Core problem**: AI references are too "public" — famous quotes, classic films, universally known memes. Real cultural references have a barrier to entry, carry the smell of a specific era, and require decoding.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- Assign characters era- and subculture-specific reference frames based on age, job, and region
- Characters don't explain their references — explaining a reference kills it
- Generational gaps are conflict material: one character's shorthand is another's gibberish

### 7. Language Quirks — Every Voice Has Its Own Rough Edges

**Core problem**: AI gives different characters the same voice — fluent, complete, grammatically correct prose. Real people talk in fragments, tics, and preferred structures.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- Assign each major character 1–2 language markers: a catchphrase, a signature sentence structure, a dialect word, a metaphor habit
- Language markers must be consistent — once set, carry them through
- Realistic dialogue includes "filler": well, uh, I mean, you know, like

**❌ Typical AI**:
> "I believe this plan carries certain risks," Zhang Wei said. "We need to reconsider."
> "I understand your concerns," Li Hua replied, "but we have no choice."

**✅ Human texture**:
> "This is — no, listen," Zhang Wei jabbed his chopsticks at the table, "you keep doing this, it's gonna blow up."
> Li Hua picked up a piece of meat and chewed for a while before saying: "So what do you want to do." Not a question.

### 8. Time-Space Anchors — Pin Scenes to Real Coordinates

**Core problem**: AI scene-setting often floats in mid-air — like a server with no IP address: you know it's running but not where.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- Every scene needs at least one geographic detail you could mark on a map, or a time coordinate you could look up
- Anchor time through events, not years: "the year of SARS," "right when ride-sharing apps launched," "the summer the factory closed"
- Use the names locals actually use, not guidebook descriptions

### 9. Irregular Rhythm — Sentence Shape Is Emotion Itself

**Core problem**: AI sentence patterns are like a metronome — even, stable, predictable. Human writing breathes: panting when tense, stretching when calm.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- Tense scenes: fragments. Short bursts. Single words as sentences. No complete thoughts.
- Calm scenes: long sentences nesting clause within clause, the way a river bends
- Deliberately create extreme contrast between long and short sentences within the same paragraph
- Paragraph length is rhythm too: a sudden one-sentence paragraph is a rest note in music

**❌ Typical AI**:
> He walked quickly toward the door and pushed open the heavy iron gate. Cold wind hit his face, carrying the scent of rain-soaked earth. He pulled his coat tight and headed into the alley.

**✅ Human texture**:
> He ran.
>
> The gate didn't close behind him — forget it — cold air down his collar, still raining, shoe straight into a puddle, sock soaked through, but he didn't stop. Light at the end of the alley. Maybe a streetlamp. Maybe a convenience store. Maybe he was seeing things.

### 10. Real Vulnerability — Uncertainty Itself Is Honesty

**Core problem**: AI is confident and complete. Every sentence reads like a manual. But real people — especially at important moments — are full of hesitation and self-doubt.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- A character's inner monologue at key decisions should be messy and self-contradictory
- "I don't know" is a powerful line — don't rush to give characters answers
- Let characters keep doubting themselves after a decision is made — that's more real than conviction

### 11. Humor and Irony — The Joke Must Grow from the Situation

**Core problem**: AI humor is "arranged" — wordplay, deliberate contrast, engineered cuteness. Real humor is rooted in situation; sometimes even the writer doesn't realize what's funny.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- The best humor comes from a character's blind spot — they're dead serious and the reader can't help laughing
- Irony comes from information gaps: the character doesn't know what the reader knows, or says one thing while doing the opposite
- Humor doesn't need the narrator to point it out — if you have to explain why it's funny, it isn't

### 12. Process Trace — Keep the Rough Edges of Thinking

**Core problem**: AI output reads like a hardcover edition — too clean, too complete, too "finished." Real writing preserves traces of the thinking process.

**How to implement in fiction**:
- Occasionally let a self-questioning voice appear in the narration: "Maybe that's not how it happened"
- Description doesn't always have to be precise — "maybe twenty people, could've been thirty, the room was full anyway"
- Scene transitions don't need to be smooth — an abrupt jump sometimes feels more like real memory than a crafted bridge

## Self-Check Checklist

After writing a passage, do a quick scan:

- [ ] Can you tell at a glance where and when this scene takes place? (Time-space anchors)
- [ ] Does the character feel only one emotion? Is that believable? (Emotional granularity)
- [ ] Are sensory descriptions specific enough to smell and touch? (Sensory detail)
- [ ] If you cover the names, can you tell which character is speaking? (Language quirks)
- [ ] Is the narration too smooth, too balanced, too correct? (Cognitive imperfection / POV bias)
- [ ] Do sentence lengths vary? Is there a mix of breathless long sentences and sudden short ones? (Irregular rhythm)
- [ ] In the last three paragraphs, is there any "pretty filler" that only AI would write? Delete it.
