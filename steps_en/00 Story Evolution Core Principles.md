# Story Evolution Core Principles (mandatory for all plot-planning stages)

Internalize these principles. When designing plot, do not quote them, do not list them as a checklist — let them become your intuition when making decisions.

---

## Core Formula

The only legitimate method of story progression:

**Character makes a choice → Choice produces a cost → Cost changes the situation → New situation forces the next choice**

Every beat, every chapter, every act runs this chain. Break the chain and the story dies.

---

## Five Driving Engines

Plot progression must grow from the following sources — not be "arranged" from outside by the author:

### 1. Character Desire and Personality Flaw

A character wants something, and a flaw in their personality happens to block the most direct path to getting it. Desire provides thrust; the flaw creates detours. Remove either one and the character becomes a puppet pushed by the plot.

**Test**: If you replaced this character with someone else, would the plot develop exactly the same way? If yes, the character is not truly driving the story.

### 2. Conflict of Interest and Information Gap

What different characters want is incompatible, or they each made a "reasonable" decision based on different information. The best conflicts have neither side wrong — just different positions, different information, different value hierarchies.

**Test**: Can you explain in one sentence why this character absolutely had to do it this way? If you cannot, either the motivation is not written clearly enough, or this conflict was manufactured rather than organic.

### 3. Existing Contradictions Fermenting

Old rifts do not resolve themselves. Every "temporary shelving" accumulates pressure until some tiny trigger ignites the entire crack. Problems planted in the first half of the story must pay off in the second half — not be forgotten, not be "resolved" perfunctorily.

**Test**: Looking back at the last three beats, which storyline was quietly dropped? A dropped contradiction is one the reader will remember.

### 4. Event Consequences Accumulating

Every action a character takes leaves a trace. Kill a person and someone will come to take revenge — or to give thanks. Tell a lie and someone will quote that lie later. The world has memory — consequences do not disappear, they only transform.

**Test**: What major decision did the character make in the last beat? Are the consequences of that decision reflected in the current beat? If not, either the consequences are coming too slowly (the reader has already forgotten), or you skipped writing them.

### 5. Situational Pressure Escalating

The stakes get higher, the escape routes narrower, the time tighter. At each beat, the character's situation should be harder than the last — not linearly harder, but "every time a problem is solved, a bigger one is exposed."

**Test**: Compare the character's situation now with three beats ago. Is it harder or easier? If it is easier, you may be giving the protagonist a golden finger.

## Prioritize These ("slow-cooked flavor is worth the time")

When writing plot, prioritize these "low-intensity but high-tension" elements:

| Element | Why prioritize | How |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Latent crisis** | Readers see danger the character doesn't yet — this tortures more than when danger actually arrives | Give readers information the character doesn't have; let readers worry on the character's behalf |
| **Pressure accumulation** | Pressure itself is narrative momentum — it holds attention without needing to erupt | Let the character's daily routine get progressively harder — small troubles stack, normal flow starts jamming |
| **Hidden fractures** | A relationship looks fine on the surface but the foundation is cracking — when it breaks, the impact is far greater than if it had been opposed from the start | Let allies develop small disagreements, temporarily shelved, but the shelving itself accumulates resentment |
| **Situational deterioration** | No new events needed — just let old events' consequences start to materialize | The fallout from some "small problem" quietly expanded its scope |
| **Irreversible consequences** | Once past this point, there's no going back — this is where the story gains weight | The character does something that cannot be taken back (said something irrevocable, hurt someone who cannot be healed, gave up an opportunity that cannot be reclaimed) |

**Do not rush into high-intensity pay-offs too early.** The power of an explosive scene depends on how much potential was accumulated beforehand. An explosion without setup is just noise.

## Seven Prohibitions

Each of these is a mistake AI writing makes most often — and the easiest reason readers drop a book.

### ❌ 1. Forcing Escalation for Stimulation

**What it means**: The character had no reason to do something, but the author needed the plot to move forward, so the character "suddenly decided."

**How to spot it**: If you delete this event, does the logic before and after still hold? If it does — even if it's smoother — this was force-fitted.

**Correct approach**: Go back to the last beat, plant the cause there, and let the current event become the inevitable effect.

### ❌ 2. Character Intelligence Drop to Push Plot

**What it means**: A smart character suddenly becomes foolish, a cautious character suddenly reckless, an experienced character suddenly forgets common sense — only because the author needed them to make a mistake.

**How to spot it**: Would this character have made the same decision in the previous scene? If the previous scene clearly shows they wouldn't, that is a dumbing-down.

**Correct approach**: Let the character make a decision that seems "reasonable from their perspective" — a wrong decision born from information gaps or personality flaws, not from sudden stupidity.

### ❌ 3. Reward Without Cost

**What it means**: The character gains something important (power, an ally, information, a turning point) but pays nothing for it.

**How to spot it**: What did the character gain? What did they give up for it? If the answer is "nothing," it was a freebie.

**Correct approach**: Every gain comes with a loss. The cost of power might be health, relationships, moral position, or time. The bigger the gain, the heavier the cost must be.

### ❌ 4. Reversal Without Setup

**What it means**: A truth is suddenly revealed that had zero foreshadowing, or the situation reverses with zero warning.

**How to spot it**: Looking back at the earlier text, can readers find at least two clues pointing to this reversal? If they can't find even one, this is ambushing the reader, not a reversal.

**Correct approach**: The rule for reversals is "it all makes sense looking back, but nobody noticed it at the time." Plant the clues first (at least two, scattered across different scenes), then detonate.

### ❌ 5. Emotion and Relationship Instant Extremity

**What it means**: Two characters go from strangers to deep love in one scene, or from trust to betrayal in one page.

**How to spot it**: Expand the relationship change into a timeline — are there enough intermediate states in the middle? Does human emotion have gradients?

**Correct approach**: Every relationship advancement needs a "catalytic event" — not any event will do, but the one that can specifically pry open the current relationship state. From stranger to trust requires: shared danger → being supported at a key moment → discovering something nobody else knows about the other → choosing the other despite a conflict of interest. Skip any stage and the reader won't buy it.

### ❌ 6. World Rules Failing for Convenience

**What it means**: World settings established earlier (physical rules, magic systems, social systems) suddenly stop working when needed, or a rule that was never mentioned before suddenly appears.

**How to spot it**: Could this solution have been used three chapters ago? If yes, why wasn't it used then?

**Correct approach**: Once established, world rules are rigid constraints. If a new rule is needed to advance the plot, it must be naturally revealed in the story at least one beat in advance (through character discovery, old documents, others' experiences, etc.).

### ❌ 7. Continuous High-Intensity Bombardment

**What it means**: Every chapter is a climax, every scene is a life-or-death moment, characters are always at maximum tension — and the reader becomes numb.

**How to spot it**: Among the last three consecutive beats, is there any room to "catch your breath"? Any daily life, humor, warmth, or reflection?

**Correct approach**: Rhythm is wave-shaped. After a climax there must be a fall — the fall is not boredom, it is the reader digesting what just happened, the character processing the emotional aftermath, the next climax having room to build落差. A silent look exchanged might be more powerful than a fight — but only if it appears in the right place.

## Evolution Rhythm Reference

A healthy story evolution rhythm looks roughly like this:

```
Buildup → Accumulation → Accumulation → Small eruption → Consequence → New buildup → Accumulation → Accumulation → Accumulation → Big eruption → Deep consequence → Breath → ...
```

Note:
- Accumulation phases should outnumber eruption phases — most of the篇幅 goes to "things getting progressively worse"
- Every eruption must be followed by a consequence-handling phase — no jumping to the next storyline after an explosion
- Eruption intensity should escalate act by act — the small eruption of Act 3 should be more intense than the big eruption of Act 1
- The breath phase is not "nothing happening" — it is "characters digesting, relationships reorganizing, readers anticipating"

## Self-Check: Ask Yourself After Every Beat

1. **Causality**: Is this event a necessary consequence of the previous event? If you deleted the previous event, would this one still happen?
2. **Character**: Did the character's choice drive this event, or did the author's need?
3. **Cost**: What did the character gain in this beat? What did they pay? Are the two roughly equivalent?
4. **Escalation**: Is the character's situation harder than the last beat? Are their escape routes narrower?
5. **Setup**: In this beat, did you plant at least one line for the next 2–3 beats?
6. **Payoff**: How many of the lines planted earlier were paid off in this beat? Were any old lines forgotten?
7. **Rhythm**: Is the intensity of this beat rising or falling compared to the adjacent beats? Has there been a continuous run in the same direction?
