## Core Task

Based on the creative proposal, design the world where this story takes place — not an encyclopedia entry, but an answer to one question: **How does this world make the protagonist's situation harder, choices more painful, and costs more real?**

Good worldbuilding serves conflict. Every setting should answer: "If this rule exists, which of the character's choices becomes harder?" If you can't answer that, the setting is decoration — cut it.

---

## Output Constraints

- The whole document should stay within 1800–3000 characters, with a hard cap of 3500 characters
- Each dimension should contain 3–5 key entries, each entry 1–3 sentences
- Only keep settings that affect conflict, character action, social resistance, or the texture of the genre
- No encyclopedia-style history, no geographic detail with no plot function, no pure atmospheric prose

---

## Boundary Constraints

Worldbuilding only describes "what kind of world this is," and does not involve specific events that happen in the story:

- ❌ Do not design specific plot events (e.g., "a certain case occurs")
- ❌ Do not design protagonist actions or decisions
- ❌ Do not design conflict events between characters
- ❌ Do not design the ending direction
- ✅ Only describe the world's background rules, operating logic, environmental traits, and sources of pressure

---

## Design Dimensions

### 1. Time-Space Background

- Era coordinate: what era, what season, what level of technology
- Time pressure: what is countdowning in this era, what window is closing
- Core settings: the primary spaces where the story mainly takes place, how these spaces restrict or amplify conflict
- Spatial rules: where characters can and cannot go, how distance and movement create difficulties

### 2. Power and Society

- Power structure: who is at the top of the food chain, how resources are distributed, what the上升通道 looks like
- Unspoken rules: the gap between surface rules and how things actually work — this gap is where story lives
- Class friction: the specific points of interest conflict between different classes/factions
- Information barriers: who knows what, who is kept in the dark, how information asymmetry creates misjudgments

### 3. Core Rules

- World hard rules: the iron laws that cannot be violated in this world (physical / magical / social / legal)
- Cost of rules: what must be sacrificed to use a certain power/resource/privilege
- Rule boundaries: under what boundary conditions do rules失效 or get exploited
- Forbidden zones: what is absolutely forbidden to do, and what irreversible consequence follows

### 4. Daily Texture

- Sensory anchors: what this world smells/sounds/feels like (3–5 concrete details)
- Daily rhythm: how ordinary people get through a day, what is mundane and what draws attention
- Cultural symbols: what people of this world use to express identity, loyalty, hostility, or intimacy
- Danger signals: what detail appearing means trouble is coming

### 5. Story Utility

- Natural conflict sources: which contradictions are already embedded in the world's structure (no additional event design needed; the contradictions are already baked in)
- Escalation space: how the world's complexity can support the story growing larger without collapsing
- Genre texture: what is the core reading experience this world gives readers (tense / mysterious / oppressive / rousing / absurd…)
