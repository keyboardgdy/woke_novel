## Core of Writing

It is not "repeating the plot."

Enter the character's state in this moment.

Characters do not act to complete the plot. They act because they are:

- Responding to emotion
- Responding to desire
- Responding to pressure
- Responding to relationship change

The novel's draft is simply what naturally falls out of the character's behavior.

## Core Goal

Make the reader feel:

> "These characters are alive."

Not:

> "The author is arranging the plot."

> Core attention: every scene must change the world. Emotion -> emotion -> emotion is forbidden.

## Storyline Principles (most important)

Every scene must clearly answer:

```
Who is doing what?
Why are they doing it?
What is the result?
```

Forbidden:
- Pure emotional flow (happy -> sad -> angry -> calm)
- Characters waiting for things to happen
- Events happening TO the character rather than the character producing them
- Atmosphere description standing in for story progression

Allowed:
- Characters actively investigating / probing / avoiding / concealing / attacking
- Characters making choices out of desire or fear
- The world reacting to a character's action
- Characters forced to respond to someone else's action (but they must show HOW they respond)

---

## Scene Principles

Every scene must contain all of these at once:

- Character goal
- Emotional pressure
- Hidden desire
- Relationship change
- A push on relationship debt or intimacy boundary

Even a quiet scene must produce a small relationship change. The change can be:

- Closing / pulling away
- Defensive / wavering
- Misunderstanding / probing
- Dependence / suppression / loss of control
- Care / refusing care / almost crossing the line / stepping back right after crossing it

## Romantic Charge, Abstract Intimacy, and Sexual Intimacy Principles

The romantic charge between a man and a woman should be written through concrete intimate actions and relationship change first, not as emotional labels. When the plot, character relationship, and consent conditions are in place, sexual intimacy can happen in real time, and the draft does not need to dodge it on purpose.

Allowed and encouraged: describing the sexual attraction of adult men or women. Attractiveness is not a label, but a shift of attention and a bodily reaction that a person causes in another character's view.

Attractiveness can come from:

- Posture, gait, the way they sit or stand, the controlled or loose quality of their motion
- Lowered voice, breathing changes, pauses, the hint of a smile, the distance at which they speak
- The way clothing fits, damp hair, sweat, wounds, body temperature, scent, skin state
- Sense of power, softness, danger, maturity, wildness, restraint, fragility, or contrast
- The person being seen knows their own appeal and uses it, or doesn't know and hits harder

How to write it:

- Write from the attracted party's view: what they see, why they look away, where the body reacts first, how their actions slow or get clumsy.
- Pick only 1-3 attractive details with the most narrative function each time. Do not scan the whole body evenly.
- The attractive detail must change the dialogue, action, distance, initiative, or psychological defense.
- You can write male appeal and female appeal alike. Do not default to only women being watched; men also need bodily presence and sexual appeal.

You may use:

- Adjusting clothing, returning a personal object, shielding the other from an awkward moment
- Treating a wound, checking a temperature, sharing an object that holds warmth
- Forced closeness from a rainy night, cold, narrow space, or a danger-ruse disguise
- Reaching out and stopping, touching and pulling back too fast
- Letting the gaze land where it shouldn't, then covering it with a different line
- Doing something that is clearly care, but framing it as an order, a transaction, something done in passing, or an irritation

Every intimate moment must have:

1. A justified reason to approach: why they must be close right now.
2. A boundary that stops them: identity, shame, danger, pride, misreading, or an old wound that won't let them continue.
3. A reaction after: pulling back, talking tough, changing the subject, going cold, seizing the initiative, going silent, getting clumsy.

An intimate action must change at least one of these in the relationship: initiative, trust, defensiveness, debt, misunderstanding, dependence.

When sexual intimacy actually happens:

- Both characters must be adult, sober, and willing.
- Consent can be implicit, but it must be readable. If one side hesitates, refuses, or asks to stop, the other must respect it.
- You may explicitly acknowledge that the two of them had sexual intimacy. You may write the process without details, but you cannot use a blank jump-cut to pretend it didn't happen.
- Place descriptive focus on choice, approach, pause, breath, bodily aftermath, shame, defense, confirmation, the silence after, and relationship change.
- Sexual intimacy must carry consequences: trust shift, dependence shift, power-balance shift, added debt, misunderstanding resolved or deepened, self-knowledge changed, downstream risk introduced.

Forbidden:

- Using the labels "ambiguity, heart-flutter, desire, pulse-quickening, feelings blooming in silence" in place of concrete action.
- Using only "very beautiful, very handsome, very sexy, seductive, hot" in place of concrete sexual appeal.
- Writing attractiveness as a full-body scan, a body-part checklist, or a gaze unrelated to the character's situation.
- Letting the two sides immediately clear things up, confirm the relationship, or speak every feeling aloud.
- Sacrificing a character's boundaries, judgment, or dignity for romantic charge.
- Writing intimacy as offense, coercion,失控 possession, or consequence-free atmosphere play.
- Writing sexual intimacy as organs, positions, steps, or a technical checklist.
- Sexual intimacy happening with no aftermath, no changed person, no relationship consequence.

## Character Principles

Characters do not stay locked in one state.

Characters are allowed to:

- Talk tough / lose control / fall silent / regret
- Probe / change the subject / say the wrong thing
- Force themselves to look calm
- Attack out of shame
- Flee out of fear

Do not make every character forever restrained, forever deep, forever "premium casting."

Real people are messy.

---

## Emotional Expression Principles

Do not explain emotions directly.

Prefer:

- Action / breath / pause
- Gaze / shifts in speech tempo
- Shifts in attention / bodily reaction
- Unusual attention to the environment

to convey the character's state.

But do not mechanically forbid interiority. Brief, real, natural inner reactions are allowed.

Avoid the authorial summary:

Wrong:
- "He finally grew up"
- "She realized she had fallen in love with him"
- "Their relationship had moved one step closer"

Right:
- "He suddenly didn't dare look at her"
- "She wanted to say something, then swallowed it back"

---

## Dialogue Principles

Dialogue is not information transfer. It is:

- Concealment / probing / defense
- Push and pull / emotional release / seeking a response

Allowed:

- Small talk / pauses / being cut off
- Answers that miss the point / repetition / emotionalized language

Do not make every line a "golden line."

---

## Pacing Principles

Do not stay in constant high pressure, depth, oppression, or literary feel.

Allowed:

- Loosening / trivial moments / everyday texture
- Brief lightness / meaninglessness

Pacing must breathe.

---

## Subtext Principles

Characters do not always hide what they think. Some will:

- Not be able to keep it in
- Suddenly erupt
- Use attack to cover vulnerability
- Regret what they just said

Subtext is only one mode of expression. Do not keep the whole text in a state of "pressed down, unsaid."

---

## Body Sense Principles

Characters must have bodily presence. Note:

- Fatigue / pain / breath
- Hunger / temperature
- Muscle reaction / conditioned reflex

Non-human characters must keep:

- Wildness / animality / instinct / aggression

Do not write every character as a "cool, restrained literary-human character."

---

## Scene Hidden Drive

Every scene must contain a hidden drive:

```yaml
scene_hidden_drive:
  role_a:
    suppress: ""   # what they won't admit
    fear: ""       # what they're afraid of losing
    desire: ""     # what they really want

  role_b:
    suppress: ""
    fear: ""
    desire: ""
```

Do not write it out directly. Let it surface naturally through behavior, pauses, tone, avoidance, defense, attack, and shifts in attention.

---

## Literary-Feel Control

Do not actively produce:

- "Premium feel" / "cinematic feel"
- "Philosophical dialogue" / "famous-line dialogue"

Literary feel should arise naturally from the character's state, relationship change, and emotional conflict.
